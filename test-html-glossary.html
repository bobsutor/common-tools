This glossary contains common terms and definitions from the areas of quantum computing quantum networking, quantum sensing, and quantum communications. It is a work in progress.

Most of the definitions come from Perplexity and the sources are noted.

|0〉

In quantum computing, “Ket 0” (written as |0〉) represents one of the two fundamental states of a qubit, analogous to the classical bit value of 0. It is a vector in a two-dimensional complex vector space, serving as one of the standard basis states for quantum computations. Mathematically, |0〉 is typically represented as a column vector with a 1 in the top position and a 0 in the bottom position. This state is orthogonal to its counterpart, |1〉, and together they form the computational basis for a single qubit. The |0〉 state is often used as the initial state for qubits in quantum algorithms and can be manipulated through quantum gates to create superpositions and entanglement. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 7.1 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

|1〉

In quantum computing, “Ket 1” is represented as |1〉 and denotes one of the two fundamental states of a qubit. It is part of the bra-ket notation used to describe quantum states, introduced by physicist Paul Dirac. Ket 1 corresponds to the logical 1 or “true” state in classical computing, but in the quantum realm, it represents a specific vector in a two-dimensional complex vector space. Unlike classical bits, which can only be in one state at a time, a qubit can exist in a superposition of both |0〉 and |1〉 states simultaneously. When measured, Ket 1 collapses the qubit's state, yielding a definite outcome of 1 with a probability determined by the qubit's quantum state prior to measurement. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 7.1 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

AI for quantum

AI for quantum means applying artificial intelligence to improve and manage quantum computing systems. It involves using machine learning to enhance hardware performance, correct errors, and optimize quantum algorithms. Essentially, AI is used to support and advance the capabilities of quantum computers. Source: Perplexity

Analog paradigm

The analog paradigm in quantum technologies refers to using continuous variables, such as the amplitude or phase of quantum states, to represent and process quantum information instead of discrete qubits. This approach leverages the natural, continuous nature of quantum systems to directly simulate or solve certain problems, especially in quantum simulation and optimization. Analog quantum systems excel in representing physical phenomena with continuous variables, but they are typically less flexible than digital quantum computers for general-purpose computation. Source: Perplexity

Annealing paradigm

The annealing paradigm in quantum technologies is a method for solving optimization problems by encoding them into a quantum system that evolves toward its lowest energy state, which represents the optimal or near-optimal solution. It starts with qubits in a superposition of all possible states and gradually changes the system's parameters so that it remains in or near its ground state, exploiting quantum effects like superposition, entanglement, and tunneling to explore many solutions simultaneously. This continuous-time evolution approach, known as quantum annealing, is particularly effective for complex combinatorial problems where classical methods struggle due to the vast search space. Source: Perplexity

benchmarking

Benchmarking in quantum technologies is the process of running specific tests or tasks on quantum devices to measure and evaluate their performance using defined metrics. It enables researchers to assess the quality and capabilities of quantum systems, compare different technologies, and track progress over time. Benchmarking is essential for guiding improvements and ensuring that quantum devices meet the requirements for practical applications. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 11.6 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

bit flip

A bit flip in quantum technologies refers to changing a qubit’s state from 0 to 1 or from 1 to 0. For a qubit in superposition, a bit flip swaps the probabilities of measuring 0 and 1. Bit flips are fundamental operations in quantum computing and are also a common type of error that quantum error correction codes are designed to detect and correct. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 7.6.2 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

Bloch sphere

The Bloch sphere is a three-dimensional tool used in quantum technologies to represent the state of a single qubit. The north pole of the sphere corresponds to the state ket 0 (|0〉), and the south pole corresponds to the state ket 1 (|1〉). Any other point on the sphere’s surface represents a unique pure quantum state, showing all possible superpositions between ket 0 and ket 1, with the position defined by two angles. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 7.5 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

calibration

Calibration in quantum technologies is the process of adjusting and fine-tuning the parameters of quantum devices or algorithms to ensure they operate accurately and reliably. This involves comparing the performance of quantum components, such as qubits and quantum gates, against known standards and making corrections to minimize errors caused by noise or imperfections. Regular calibration is essential because quantum systems are highly sensitive to their environment, and precise adjustments help achieve accurate results in quantum computations. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 11.6.1 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

Carbon Nanotube modality

A Carbon Nanotube modality in quantum technologies refers to the use of carbon nanotubes as one-dimensional quantum systems that can host and manipulate quantum states such as electrons, excitons, or phonons. These nanotubes enable the creation of quantum devices, including qubits and single-photon emitters, by exploiting their unique electrical, optical, and mechanical properties at the nanoscale. This modality is promising for applications in quantum computing, sensing, and communication due to the exceptional quantum coherence and controllability achievable in carbon nanotube-based systems. Source: Perplexity

characterization

Characterization in quantum technologies is the process of determining and describing the behavior, properties, and performance of quantum systems such as qubits and quantum processors. It involves using experimental data to build predictive models that help understand how quantum devices operate and identify sources of errors or unwanted effects. This process is essential for assessing device quality and guiding improvements in quantum information processing. Source: Perplexity

circuit

A circuit in quantum computing is a sequence of operations that manipulate quantum bits, or qubits, using quantum gates to perform a specific computation. These circuits guide qubits through processes like superposition and entanglement, enabling complex calculations that classical circuits cannot achieve. After processing, the final measurement of the qubits produces the output of the quantum computation. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 9.2 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

CNOT gate

A CNOT gate is a two-qubit quantum logic gate that flips the target qubit from |0〉 to |1〉 or from |1〉 to |0〉 only if the control qubit is in the state |1〉. For example, it transforms the state |10〉 into |11〉 and |11〉 into |10〉, while leaving |00〉 and |01〉 unchanged. This operation is crucial for generating entanglement between qubits in quantum circuits. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 8.3.3 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

control system

A control system in quantum computing is the combination of hardware and software that generates and delivers precise signals to manipulate and measure qubits. It acts as the interface between classical computers and quantum hardware, converting digital instructions into analog or wave control signals that perform operations on qubits. This system is essential for executing quantum algorithms, protecting qubits from noise, and ensuring accurate readout of quantum states. Source: Perplexity

controlled gate

A controlled gate is a quantum gate where the operation on a target qubit depends on the state of one or more control qubits, typically when the control qubit is in the state |1〉. The gate applies a specific transformation to the target qubit only if the control condition is met, otherwise the target qubit remains unchanged. Controlled gates are essential for building complex quantum circuits and enabling entanglement between qubits. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 8.3.4 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

Digital paradigm

The digital paradigm in quantum computing refers to processing information using discrete units, such as qubits, where operations are performed through a sequence of well-defined quantum gates. This approach is similar to classical digital computing, which manipulates bits using logical gates, but in quantum systems, it leverages quantum properties like superposition and entanglement. The digital paradigm enables the construction of complex quantum algorithms by combining sequences of these gate operations on qubits. Source: Perplexity

entangled photon source

An entangled photon source is a device that generates pairs of photons whose quantum states are linked, meaning the state of one instantly affects the state of the other, no matter the distance between them. These sources are essential for quantum computing and communication, enabling secure data transfer and powerful computational processes. The generation of entangled photons underpins many quantum technologies, including quantum cryptography and quantum networks. Source: Perplexity

entangled photons

Entangled photons are pairs of light particles whose properties, such as polarization or phase, are intrinsically connected so that measuring one instantly determines the state of the other, no matter how far apart they are. This quantum link is a key resource in quantum computing and technologies, enabling tasks like secure communication and quantum information processing. The phenomenon arises from quantum mechanics and cannot be explained by classical physics. Source: Perplexity

entanglement

Entanglement is a phenomenon in quantum computing where two or more qubits become linked so that the state of one instantly determines the state of the others, no matter how far apart they are. This connection means the qubits cannot be described independently, allowing them to share and process information collectively in ways impossible for classical systems. Entanglement is essential for enabling quantum computers to perform complex calculations and achieve computational advantages over traditional computers. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 8.2 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

error correction

Error correction in quantum computing refers to techniques that protect quantum information from errors caused by noise and decoherence. This is achieved by encoding the information of a single logical qubit into multiple physical qubits, enabling the detection and correction of errors without directly measuring the quantum state. Effective error correction is essential for reliable and large-scale quantum computations. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 11.5 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

error mitigation

Error mitigation in quantum computing refers to techniques that reduce the impact of errors and noise in quantum computations, making the results more accurate and reliable. These methods do not eliminate errors entirely but use strategies like running multiple versions of a quantum circuit and post-processing the results to estimate what the error-free output should be. Error mitigation is essential for improving the usefulness of current quantum hardware before full error correction becomes practical. Source: Perplexity

error suppression

Error suppression in quantum computing refers to techniques designed to prevent errors from occurring in quantum systems by making the hardware and control methods more resistant to noise and interference. These methods operate at the most fundamental hardware level, using advanced control strategies to reduce the likelihood of errors during computation and storage. While error suppression cannot eliminate all types of errors, it significantly improves the reliability and stability of quantum operations by minimizing error rates before they happen. Source: Perplexity

fault tolerance

Fault tolerance in quantum computing is the ability of a quantum system to perform accurate computations even when errors occur in its qubits or operations. It relies on techniques like quantum error correction to detect and fix errors without disturbing the encoded quantum information. Achieving fault tolerance is essential for running complex quantum algorithms reliably, as it prevents errors from spreading and compromising the results. Source: Perplexity

Flux superconducting qubit

A flux superconducting qubit is a tiny loop of superconducting material interrupted by Josephson junctions, allowing it to act as a quantum bit. When an external magnetic flux is applied, persistent currents flow in the loop in either direction, representing distinct quantum states for computation. These states can be controlled and read out by manipulating the magnetic flux or using microwave pulses. Source: Perplexity

Fluxonium superconducting qubit

A fluxonium superconducting qubit is a quantum circuit made from a capacitor, a Josephson junction, and a large superinductor, designed to store and manipulate quantum information. Its unique architecture provides enhanced protection against environmental noise, leading to longer coherence times and higher operational accuracy compared to many other superconducting qubits. Fluxonium qubits are actively researched for their potential to improve the reliability and scalability of quantum computers. Source: Perplexity

gate

A gate in quantum computing is a fundamental operation that manipulates the state of one or more qubits, enabling the execution of quantum algorithms. Unlike classical gates, quantum gates leverage properties like superposition and entanglement, allowing qubits to exist in multiple states and interact in complex ways. Each gate is mathematically represented by a unitary matrix, ensuring the operation is reversible and preserves quantum information. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 7.6 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

H or Hadamard gate

The H or Hadamard gate is a basic quantum logic gate that puts a qubit into a superposition state. It changes the state of a qubit so that it has equal probability of being measured as 0 or 1. This gate is crucial for creating quantum interference and enabling many quantum algorithms. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 7.6.5 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

HPC integration

HPC integration in the context of quantum computing refers to combining high-performance classical computing systems with quantum processors to solve complex problems more efficiently. This integration allows hybrid workflows where classical and quantum resources work together, with each handling the tasks they are best suited for, such as using quantum devices to accelerate specific computations within a larger classical framework. The approach requires specialized software, resource management, and workflow coordination to enable seamless operation between classical and quantum components. Source: Perplexity

IDE

An Integrated Development Environment, or IDE, in quantum computing is a software application that supports the creation and testing of quantum programs. It offers tools for writing code, simulating quantum circuits, and analyzing results using qubits and quantum logic. This environment streamlines the workflow for researchers and developers working on quantum technologies. Source: Perplexity

interference

In quantum computing, interference refers to the phenomenon where the probability amplitudes of quantum states combine like waves, either reinforcing (constructive interference) or canceling (destructive interference) each other. This wave-like behavior allows quantum algorithms to amplify the probability of correct answers and suppress incorrect ones during computation. By carefully designing quantum circuits, interference is harnessed to guide the outcome of calculations, providing a key advantage over classical computing. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 9.6.3 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

logical gate

The logical gate in quantum computing is designed to operate on encoded qubit states, such as |0〉 and |1〉, which are protected by quantum error correction codes. The action of a fault-tolerant quantum gate ensures that errors affecting individual physical qubits do not spread uncontrollably and can be detected and corrected without disturbing the encoded quantum information. A fault-tolerant logical gate is essential for reliable quantum computation, as it enables accurate processing of quantum information even in the presence of noise and operational imperfections. Source: Perplexity

logical qubit

The logical qubit is an abstract unit of quantum information formed by encoding a single qubit's state across multiple physical qubits using quantum error correction. This approach protects quantum data from errors and noise by allowing the detection and correction of faults in individual physical qubits. The logical qubit enables more reliable quantum computations, making it essential for building practical, large-scale quantum computers. Source: Perplexity

measurement

Measurement in quantum computing is the process of extracting classical information from quantum systems by observing qubits. When a qubit is measured, its superposition collapses into a definite state, producing a classical result that is inherently probabilistic. This act of measurement not only determines the outcome but also irreversibly alters the quantum state, making it essential for obtaining results and enabling tasks like error correction and adaptive algorithms. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 8.2.1 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

microwave photon

A microwave photon is a single quantum of electromagnetic energy in the microwave frequency range, typically used to control and manipulate qubits in quantum computing systems. It carries quantum information and can interact with quantum devices like superconducting circuits or trapped ions, enabling operations such as quantum gates and state readout. Because of its low energy and long wavelength compared to optical photons, it is especially suited for on-chip quantum information processing and memory. Source: Perplexity

modality

In quantum computing, modality refers to the specific physical approach or technology used to create, control, and measure qubits, the fundamental units of quantum information. Different modalities use various underlying systems—such as trapped ions, superconducting circuits, or photons—to realize quantum operations and algorithms. Each modality has its own strengths and challenges in terms of scalability, error rates, and suitability for different quantum computing tasks. Source: Perplexity

Neutral Atoms modality

The Neutral Atoms modality is a quantum computing approach where individual neutral atoms are used as qubits, trapped and manipulated with laser light in highly organized arrays. The atoms’ quantum states are controlled and entangled using precise laser techniques, often leveraging strong interactions in excited Rydberg states to perform quantum logic gates. This modality stands out for its scalability, flexibility in arranging qubits, and reduced susceptibility to certain errors, making it a promising platform for advancing quantum technologies. Source: Perplexity

NMR modality

The NMR modality is a quantum computing approach that uses the spin states of atomic nuclei within molecules as qubits, manipulated and read out using nuclear magnetic resonance techniques. The quantum information is processed by applying radio frequency pulses to control these nuclear spins, allowing for the implementation of quantum gates and algorithms. This modality typically operates on large ensembles of molecules, enabling proof-of-principle demonstrations of quantum algorithms with a limited number of qubits. Source: Perplexity

No-Cloning Theorem

The No-Cloning Theorem is a fundamental rule in quantum computing that states it is impossible to create an exact and independent copy of an arbitrary unknown quantum state. The theorem arises because quantum operations are linear and unitary, preventing the duplication of quantum information in the way classical data can be copied. This principle has major implications for quantum technologies, affecting error correction, cryptography, and communication protocols. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 9.3.4 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

NV Centers in Diamond modality

The NV Centers in Diamond modality is a quantum computing approach that uses special defects in a diamond lattice, where a nitrogen atom replaces a carbon atom next to a vacant site, to create qubits based on electron or nuclear spin states. These NV centers offer long coherence times and allow for the initialization, manipulation, and optical readout of quantum information, making them suitable for quantum computation, sensing, and networking. The unique properties of the diamond lattice provide physical protection and isolation, enhancing qubit stability and enabling advanced quantum technologies. Source: Perplexity

paradigm

A paradigm in quantum computing refers to a fundamental approach or model for processing and manipulating quantum information. It defines the rules, methods, and structures by which quantum systems perform computations. Different paradigms, such as gate-based or measurement-based models, guide how quantum technologies are designed and operated. Source: Perplexity

phase flip

The phase flip is a quantum operation that changes the sign of the phase of a qubit’s state without altering its probability amplitudes. This gate effectively turns a positive superposition into a negative one, flipping the relative phase between the basis states. It is fundamental in quantum error correction and certain quantum algorithms. Source: Perplexity

photon

A photon is a fundamental particle of light that carries energy and has no mass. In quantum computing, photons are used to encode and transmit quantum information as qubits. The use of photons enables fast, low-loss communication and processing in optical quantum technologies. Source: Perplexity

photon detector

A photon detector is a device used to sense and measure individual photons, the fundamental particles of light. In quantum computing and technologies, it plays a critical role in reading out quantum information carried by photons. These detectors enable precise control and measurement in quantum communication, sensing, and computing systems. Source: Perplexity

photon source

A photon source is a device or system that generates single photons or controlled streams of photons for use in quantum computing and quantum technologies. These photons act as carriers of quantum information, enabling secure communication and quantum logic operations. The quality and reliability of the photon source are crucial for the performance of photonic quantum systems. Source: Perplexity

Photonic modality

The photonic modality is a quantum computing approach that uses photons (particles of light) as the physical carriers of quantum information. The quantum information can be encoded in various properties of photons, such as their polarization, path, or phase, and is processed using optical elements like beam splitters and phase shifters. This modality includes both discrete-variable and continuous-variable schemes, enabling quantum operations through interference, measurement, and entanglement of photons. Source: Perplexity

physical qubit

A physical qubit is a quantum bit realized in a specific physical system, such as a trapped ion or a superconducting loop. The physical qubit can exist in a superposition of two basis states, enabling quantum computation. Multiple physical qubits are often combined and managed to create more stable logical qubits for practical quantum algorithms. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 1.6 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

pulse-level access

Pulse-level access in quantum computing allows users to directly control and customize the physical pulses that manipulate qubits, rather than relying solely on abstract gate-level instructions. This approach enables precise tuning of parameters such as pulse shape, amplitude, duration, and timing, providing deeper visibility and optimization capabilities for quantum algorithms. By working at this lower level, researchers can improve algorithm performance, enhance error correction, and address hardware-specific challenges in quantum systems. Source: Perplexity

QIR standard developer

The QIR Alliance (Quantum Intermediate Representation Alliance) is a community-driven initiative focused on creating an interoperable specification for quantum programs. Its goal is to enable seamless use of different quantum devices by developing technology that supports diverse quantum processors. The alliance brings together industry and academic partners to advance compiler technology for quantum programming languages and execution systems. Source: Perplexity

quantum for AI

Quantum for AI refers to using quantum computing to enhance artificial intelligence and machine learning. The unique properties of quantum mechanics allow quantum computers to process information in fundamentally new ways compared to classical computers. This could help AI systems learn more efficiently, discover patterns more quickly, and solve complex optimization problems that are challenging for traditional computers. Quantum algorithms may eventually enable faster training of AI models and improve their accuracy by exploring many possibilities at once. The ongoing development of quantum technology has the potential to make AI smarter, faster, and more powerful than ever before. Source: Perplexity

qubit

A qubit is the core building block of quantum computing that stores quantum information. Unlike a classical bit, a qubit can represent 0, 1, or any combination of both simultaneously through superposition. This ability allows quantum technologies to process and analyze information in fundamentally new ways. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 7.1 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

S gate

The S gate is a single-qubit quantum gate that applies a phase shift of π/2 radians to the |1〉 state. It leaves the state |0〉 unchanged and transforms |1〉 to i|1〉. For a general state α|0〉 + β|1〉, the S gate produces α|0〉 + iβ|1〉. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 7.6.7 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

SDK

The SDK, or software development kit, in quantum computing is a software framework that provides developers with tools and libraries to build, test, and deploy quantum algorithms and applications. The SDK typically includes simulators, compilers, and APIs, allowing users to write quantum code and run it on either simulators or actual quantum hardware. By abstracting complex hardware details, the SDK enables easier and more accessible development of quantum technologies. Source: Perplexity

Silicon Spin modality

The Silicon Spin modality is a quantum computing approach that uses the spin of single electrons or nuclei trapped in silicon quantum dots or donor atoms to encode and manipulate quantum information. This modality leverages established semiconductor fabrication techniques, enabling precise control of individual spins and the construction of scalable quantum circuits. Silicon spin qubits are notable for their long coherence times and high-fidelity gate operations, making them a leading candidate for fault-tolerant quantum computing. Source: Perplexity

simulation and emulation

The simulation of quantum computing is the process of using classical computers to mathematically model the behavior of quantum systems or algorithms, allowing researchers to test and develop quantum software without requiring actual quantum hardware. An emulation in quantum technologies refers to running quantum algorithms on classical hardware in a way that mimics the specific characteristics and limitations of real quantum computers, sometimes including noise and hardware-specific imperfections. Both approaches are essential for prototyping, debugging, and validating quantum algorithms before deploying them on actual quantum devices. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 11.8 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

Superconducting modality

The superconducting modality is a quantum computing approach that uses circuits made from superconducting materials to create and control qubits. These qubits exploit the properties of superconductivity and Josephson junctions, allowing fast quantum operations through the manipulation of quantized energy states with microwave pulses. This modality is valued for its scalability, rapid gate speeds, and compatibility with established microfabrication techniques, making it a leading platform in current quantum technology research and development. Source: Perplexity

Superfluid Helium modality

The Superfluid Helium modality is an approach in quantum computing that uses electrons trapped on the surface of superfluid helium to serve as quantum bits (qubits). The superfluid helium provides an exceptionally clean and stable environment, enabling electrons to maintain their quantum properties for extended periods and allowing for precise control of their quantum states. This modality leverages the unique quantum mechanical behaviors of superfluid helium to achieve long coherence times and potentially high-performance quantum operations. Source: Perplexity

superposition

Superposition is a key quantum phenomenon where a quantum bit can exist in a blend of both 0 and 1 states simultaneously, described by a mathematical combination called a quantum state. This means a quantum system can represent and process many possible outcomes at once, rather than just one at a time. The use of superposition in quantum computing enables powerful parallelism, forming the basis for speedups in certain algorithms and technologies. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 7.1 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

Topological modality

The topological modality is a quantum computing approach that encodes information in the global, topological properties of certain exotic states of matter, using quasiparticles called anyons. Quantum gates are performed by braiding these anyons, making computations inherently resistant to local errors and environmental noise. This modality aims to achieve robust, fault-tolerant quantum operations by leveraging the stability of topological states rather than relying on traditional error correction methods. Source: Perplexity

Transmon superconducting qubit

The transmon superconducting qubit is a type of quantum bit based on a superconducting circuit containing a Josephson junction shunted by a large capacitor, which creates discrete energy levels for storing and manipulating quantum information. It is specifically engineered to be less sensitive to charge noise, resulting in improved stability and longer coherence times compared to earlier superconducting qubits. The transmon design is widely used in quantum computing because it enables reliable control of quantum states using microwave pulses and is scalable for building larger quantum processors. Source: Perplexity

Trapped Ions modality

The trapped ions modality in quantum computing uses charged atomic particles confined and suspended by electromagnetic fields as qubits. Quantum information is encoded in the ions' internal electronic states, and quantum gates are implemented by manipulating these states and their collective vibrational modes with lasers. This approach offers high-fidelity operations and long coherence times, making it a promising architecture for scalable, universal quantum computers. Source: Perplexity

X gate

The X gate is a fundamental single-qubit quantum gate that acts as a quantum version of the classical NOT gate. It flips the state of a qubit from | 0 〉 |0〉 to | 1 〉 |1〉, and from | 1 〉 |1〉 to | 0 〉 |0〉. When applied to a superposition state, the X gate swaps the amplitudes of | 0 〉 |0〉 and | 1 〉 |1〉. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 7.6.2 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

Y gate

The Y gate is a fundamental single-qubit quantum gate that applies a specific rotation to a qubit’s state in the computational basis, represented using ket notation as |0〉 and |1〉. When the Y gate acts on a qubit, it transforms |0〉 into i|1〉 and |1〉 into -i|0〉, where i is the imaginary unit. This operation combines a bit-flip and a phase-flip, making it distinct from other basic quantum gates. Source: Perplexity

Discussed in section 7.6.4 of Dancing with Qubits, Second Edition.

Z gate

The Z gate is a single-qubit quantum gate that applies a phase flip to a qubit. When the Z gate acts on the basis state |0〉, the state remains |0〉, but when it acts on |1〉, the state becomes −|1〉. For a superposed state a|0〉 + b|1〉, the Z gate transforms it to a|0〉 − b|1〉, flipping the sign of the |1〉 component while leaving the |0〉 component unchanged. Source: Perplexity
